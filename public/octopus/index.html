Meet the Pearl Octopus

Deep, deep down in the cold Pacific — about two miles below the waves — lives a shy little octopus the color of a pale purple pearl. Thousands of them gather in one secret spot to raise their babies. Here is the true story, told with real photos.

🫧 A grapefruit in the dark

The pearl octopus (scientists call it Muusoctopus robustus) is only about the size of a grapefruit. It has soft, pale-purple skin and no ink sac — there is no need to hide in ink when you live in near-total darkness. It roams the muddy deep-sea floor of the northeast Pacific, from Oregon all the way down to Baja California, hunting small fish and shellfish and tidying up whatever scraps drift down from above.

  • 🐙 About grapefruit-sized
  • 💜 Pale purple
  • 🌊 1,200–3,900 m deep
  • 📍 Oregon → Baja California

🏡 The Octopus Garden

In 2018, explorers guiding a robot submarine over an old underwater volcano called Davidson Seamount — about 80 miles off the coast of central California — found something no one had ever seen. Thousands upon thousands of octopuses, huddled together on the seafloor. Scientists now think more than 20,000 pearl octopuses gather there. It is the largest known gathering of octopuses anywhere on Earth, and they nicknamed it the “Octopus Garden.”

The nesting mothers, curled upside-down, shimmer in the dark like a field of pearls — which is how the octopus got its name.

🔥 Why so many? A warm secret

For a long time the gathering was a mystery. Why would shy, solitary octopuses crowd into one spot? The answer, discovered by scientists at the Monterey Bay Aquarium Research Institute, is hidden in the rock: warm water seeps up through cracks in the old volcano, like a gentle deep-sea hot spring.

Down there the sea is a frigid 35°F, but inside those warm crevices it reaches about 51°F. That warmth is the whole secret. It speeds up how fast the eggs grow — turning what could be many years of waiting into under two — so far more babies survive to hatch.

  • 🌡️ Sea around: ~35°F
  • ♨️ Warm cracks: ~51°F
  • 🥚 Eggs grow faster & safer

💜 A mother’s whole life

A pearl octopus mother gives everything for her babies. She fastens her eggs to the rock, turns herself upside down, and wraps around them to keep them safe. Then she waits — for nearly two years — guarding, cleaning, and gently blowing fresh water over the eggs. She never leaves and never eats again. When at last the tiny octopuses hatch and drift away, her long watch is over. It is one of the most devoted acts of motherhood in all the ocean.

The whole Octopus Garden is now protected inside the Monterey Bay National Marine Sanctuary, so these gentle mothers can keep their watch in peace.
